01

Why Uber Is a Unique A/B Testing Playground

Most stats classes teach A/B testing as if you have one population: "some users see version A, others see version B, look at the means." Uber breaks this because it is a three-sided marketplace with two distinct user populations whose actions affect each other in real time.

The Three Sides You Must Mention
  • Riders (demand) — care about price, ETA, ride quality.
  • Drivers (supply) — care about earnings per hour, downtime, surge predictability.
  • Uber as the platform — cares about contribution margin per trip, retention on both sides, marketplace efficiency (how often a request gets matched).

If the interviewer asks "would you launch X?", your structured answer must show how X affects EACH of the three sides. Andy Walner's Medium piece calls this out explicitly: "address specifically how a change would affect supply (drivers), demand (riders), and Uber's bottom-line."

Why Vanilla A/B Tests Break Here — and Where Your DSC 152 Knowledge Wins

Three things make Uber's experimentation harder than a textbook A/B test:

  1. SUTVA violation. If you give 50% of riders in San Diego a discount, you bid drivers AWAY from the control 50%. Treatment leaks into control through shared driver supply. Your DSC 152 hypothesis test ASSUMES independent samples — that breaks. Mention this and you sound senior.
  2. Highly skewed metrics. Trips per user, revenue per user, surge revenue — all heavy-tailed. A few power users move the mean. This is exactly why Lab 2 had you compare the t-test to the sign test and bootstrap CI on Gamma data. You already KNOW the t-test misbehaves on small-n skewed data.
  3. Network effects. A change that increases bookings can DEGRADE the experience because surge fires more often. Looking at one metric (bookings) without a guardrail (ETA, surge frequency) is rookie behavior.

Translation: every concept you are studying for Quiz 1 has a marketplace-shaped twin in this interview. Sections 3-6 walk through the mapping concretely.

Case Walkthrough: "Should Uber Roll Out a 25% Discount in San Diego?"

This is the canonical Uber APM analytical case — pulled directly from Andy Walner's article. Below is how you walk an interviewer through it using your DSC 152 toolkit.

Prompt

"Uber wants to release a 25% discount to attract new riders in San Diego. How would you decide whether to roll it out?"

Step 1 — Clarify the goal (always do this first)

Ask: "Are we optimizing for new-rider acquisition only, or for total contribution margin? Over what time horizon — 30 days or 90 days?" This single question shows the interviewer you are not racing to numbers.

Step 2 — Hypotheses (the DSC 152 Level 3 move)

H0: 25% discount has NO effect on the chosen North Star (e.g., 30-day repeat rate of new riders).
H1: 25% discount changes the 30-day repeat rate.

Two-sided is fine — "no effect" includes "discount cannibalizes margin without lifting repeat." If the interviewer presses, switch to one-sided ("we expect lift > 0") to maximize power.

Step 3 — Pick a unit of randomization (the SUTVA fix)

Do not randomize at the rider level — drivers are shared supply. Two cleaner options:

  • Geo split. Randomize at the city or neighborhood level — San Diego ZIP codes A/B/A/B. Pro: clean SUTVA. Con: tiny sample (you might have ~30 ZIPs).
  • Switchback experiment. Run discount for an hour, off for an hour, alternating. Pro: city is its own control. Con: carryover bias (drivers who came online for the discount hour stay around).

This is exactly the "two-sample / paired" decision you learned in Level 5 of the companion — but with a marketplace twist.

Step 4 — Pick a primary metric and guardrails

RoleMetricWhy
PrimaryContribution margin per acquired rider over 30 daysDiscounts only earn back if those riders stick around.
Guardrail 1p95 ETAIf demand spikes, supply shortfall hurts ETA — bad rider experience.
Guardrail 2Driver earnings per hourIf discount lowers gross bookings per trip without volume offset, drivers earn less.
Counter% of "new" riders who are returning churned riders gaming the offerCatches a known abuse pattern.

Step 5 — Sample size and power (use the calculator below)

Suppose baseline 30-day repeat rate is 40%, and you want to detect a 2-percentage-point lift (40% → 42%). You need a LOT of riders because the lift is small relative to the noise. Your DSC 152 power formula gives you the answer — Section 7 has an interactive calculator.

Step 6 — Read the result like a statistician, decide like a PM

If the test returns a 95% CI of [+1.2 pp, +2.8 pp] for repeat rate AND no guardrail breach AND positive contribution margin, ship it. If the CI straddles zero, do NOT ship — even if the point estimate is positive. This is the connection to Level 6: a CI that contains the null IS "fail to reject."

Pro Move — Tie Back to Quiz 1 Concepts Out Loud

"Because contribution margin per rider is heavy-tailed — a few high-value riders dominate — I'd want to also report a bootstrap CI alongside the t-test, exactly because the t-test can have inflated Type I error on small-n skewed data, like we saw in Lab 2."

That single sentence shows them you understand the WHY behind A/B testing, not just the mechanics.

04

Case Walkthrough: Driver-Side Experiment — Lifting Acceptance Rate

Prompt

"Drivers in Chicago accept only 65% of incoming requests. The product team wants to redesign the request screen to push that to 75%. Design the experiment."

Where DSC 152 lights up

  • Hypothesis (Level 3): H0: p = 0.65, H1: p > 0.65 (one-sided — we are not interested in the redesign making things worse).
  • Discrete data (Level 7c): Each request is a 0/1 — Bernoulli. Acceptance count is Binomial. The "rejection cutoff" intuition transfers directly.
  • Sample size: Use a two-proportion z-test. Alternatively, simulate it (your rbinom() + prop.test() skill from Lab 2).

Marketplace-specific traps to call out

  • Acceptance can rise without driver welfare rising. A driver might accept a long pickup, realize it, then cancel. Add a cancellation counter metric.
  • Selection bias on who is exposed. If only newly-onboarded drivers see the new screen, you measure novelty effect, not the redesign.
  • Heterogeneous treatment effect. The redesign might help low-tenure drivers and hurt high-tenure drivers. Slice the analysis by tenure buckets.

If you have time — the bandit angle

Mention that for high-frequency ranking-style decisions (which UI variant to show), Uber sometimes uses contextual multi-armed bandits instead of a fixed A/B. You probably won't be asked to design one; just naming the option signals breadth.

Case Walkthrough: ETA Accuracy Improvement

Prompt

"The ETA we show riders is off by 2 minutes on average. The ML team has a new model that should fix it. How do you know if it actually worked?"

Reframe the metric

"Off by 2 minutes" can mean (a) systematic bias — we always under-promise, or (b) high variance — sometimes 0, sometimes 8. Your inference toolkit answers different questions for each:

ConcernStat tool from DSC 152Decision
Bias (mean of error ≠ 0)One-sample t-test on (predicted − actual). H0: mean = 0.Reject H0 → there IS systematic bias.

Notice you used THREE different DSC 152 ideas — t-test, bootstrap CI, sign test — to triangulate one product question. That is the level of nuance that separates "knows A/B testing" from "thinks like a PM."

Enter values and click Compute.
What's Going On Under the Hood (DSC 152 Connection)

For a two-proportion z-test, sample size per arm is:

n = (z_{α/2} + z_{β})²  ·  [p₁(1−p₁) + p₂(1−p₂)]  /  (p₂ − p₁)²

where p₂ = p₁ + MDE. The z's come from qnorm() — the same family as the qt() you used to get critical values in Level 7b. Smaller MDE → bigger n. Higher power → bigger n. Higher baseline near 0.5 → bigger n (Bernoulli variance is maximized at p = 0.5).

08

Five Pitfalls That Win You "Senior-Sounding" Points

  1. Peeking. Checking the test daily and stopping when significant inflates Type I error. Mention "I'd lock the sample size up front and not look until we hit it" — or use sequential methods like alpha-spending.
  2. Multiple comparisons. If you check 20 metrics, one will look significant by chance even if nothing happened. Bonferroni or pre-register your primary metric.
  3. Novelty / primacy effects. A new UI gets a temporary lift just because it's novel; an old one gets a temporary dip during the change. Run experiments long enough to wash this out (usually 14+ days).
  4. Heterogeneous treatment effects. A change can help one segment and hurt another while the overall mean looks flat. Always slice by city, tenure, platform, and trip type.
  5. Short-term vs long-term metric divergence. Discounts boost trips this week and tank LTV next quarter. Pair every short-term primary metric with a long-term guardrail.

Mock Prompts — Practice Out Loud, Time-Boxed

Pick three. Set a 12-minute timer for each. Your goal: clarify → hypothesize → metrics → experiment design → decision rule. Click each scenario to reveal the "what good looks like" answer.

Mock 1 — Pricing

"Should Uber raise base fares 10% in Phoenix?"

Clarify: "permanently or trial?", "preserve driver pay or take more margin?". Primary: contribution margin per trip × volume = total contribution. Guardrails: rider cancellation rate, churn (rides per rider over 30 days), competitor share. Design: geo holdout — keep one comparable city as control. Watch for elasticity asymmetry: small fare hikes can disproportionately affect price-sensitive riders.
Mock 2 — Driver experience

"How would you measure success of a new driver onboarding flow?"

Define "success" = lifetime trips per driver in first 30 days × eventual retention to day 90. Input metric = % of drivers who complete onboarding. Guardrail = quality (rider rating, cancellation rate). Counter = drivers who complete onboarding but never go online. Run cohort comparison; if you A/B, randomize at the driver-applicant level (no SUTVA issue here because applicants don't share supply).
Mock 3 — Engagement

"Trips per active rider dropped 4% week over week. Diagnose."

Walk the funnel: app opens → trip requests → matches → completed trips. Where did it drop? Slice by city, platform, new vs returning. Check exogenous factors (weather, holidays, competitor promo). Confirm the drop is statistically significant — a 4% week-over-week move could be noise; bootstrap CI for trips-per-rider tells you. Tie back: "I'd compute a 95% CI for the WoW change to confirm we're not chasing noise."
Mock 4 — Safety

"How would you decide whether to launch a 'verify rider photo' feature?"

This is a risk-asymmetric launch — Type II error (missing a real safety improvement) is far more expensive than Type I (small UX friction). Pre-register a higher α (e.g., 0.10) so you have more power. Primary: incident rate per 10K trips. Guardrail: friction (request abandonment). Counter: riders gaming photo requirements. Phased rollout — small geo first, watch for unintended consequences in low-light regions.
Mock 5 — ETA prediction

"Our ETA model predicts 5 min but the actual is 7 min on average. Is the model broken?"

Don't say "yes" — first ask: "is this bias persistent, or just a recent shift?" Compare distributions, not just means. Run a one-sample t-test on (predicted − actual) per trip. If the mean is significantly negative, the model systematically under-promises. But the better PM answer: "even if it's biased, fixing the bias may not improve booking conversion — riders might prefer a slight under-promise. I'd A/B the corrected vs uncorrected ETA on conversion."

11.1 — Cohort Thinking (the gap PMs notice but APMs miss)

1 · Original idea (from the Uber guide)

Section 02 lists "repeat rate" as a metric and Section 03 picks "30-day repeat rate" as the primary metric for the discount case. But the guide never explains cohorts — how riders who joined in different weeks behave differently and why aggregate retention can lie.

2 · Simple explanation (DSC 152 style)

A cohort is just a group of users who joined at the same time. If you took your first Uber on April 1, you're in the "Apr-1 cohort." If you took your first ride on April 8, you're in the "Apr-8 cohort." Each cohort has its own retention curve.

Why this matters statistically. When you compute "retention" over all users, you are mixing cohorts. New cohorts have had less time to churn — they look "more retained" simply because the clock hasn't run out on them. This is the same kind of survivorship-style bias the DSC 152 Lab 2 sign-test discussion warns about: aggregating over a non-stationary population gives you a number that does not match what any single user experiences.

Connection to DSC 152 Level 5 ("paired vs unpaired"): a cohort comparison is essentially a paired test through time — same users, different observation windows. Aggregate retention is unpaired and loses that information.

3 · Concrete business example

You ship a referral feature on April 1. Three weeks later, the team brags: "Overall 30-day retention is up from 38% to 41%." Sounds great. But the increase is mostly because the April cohort hasn't HAD 30 days yet — only 21 days of churn opportunity instead of 30. Once you actually wait, retention drops back to 39%.

4 · What to measure (experiment-designer agent)
  • Primary metric: 30-day retention rate, computed only on cohorts that have had a full 30-day window. (Spec validated by metric_validator — see footer.)
  • Secondary: 7-day retention as an early read.
  • Guardrail: cohort size — if a week's cohort is < 1,000 riders, label the bucket "low confidence" and refuse to ship a decision on it alone.
  • Sanity check: all retention values must be in [0, 1] — verified by logical_validator.validate_ratio_bounds(), which returned 0 out-of-bounds rows on a toy 4-cohort table.
5 · How to decide

If the cohort-by-cohort retention curves separate and stay separated for at least 14 days, the lift is real. If they merge after 14 days, the early lift was a novelty effect.

6 · Common mistake

Reporting "blended" retention before all cohorts have a full window. This always inflates the latest number and tricks the team into shipping novelty. Always show retention as a cohort triangle, not a single percentage.

11.2 — Statistical Significance vs Practical Significance (the trap with huge n)

1 · Original idea (from the Uber guide)

Section 03 says "if the test returns a 95% CI of [+1.2pp, +2.8pp] — ship it." But the guide never asks the more important question: is the lift large enough to matter? At Uber's scale you can prove almost anything is statistically significant.

2 · Simple explanation (DSC 152 style)

From DSC 152 Level 7b: a t-statistic is signal divided by noise. The denominator s/√n shrinks as n grows. So with very large n, even microscopic differences clear the "is this real?" bar. Statistical significance asks "did anything change?". Practical significance asks "is the change worth a launch?". They are different questions.

3 · Concrete business example (real numbers from stats_helpers.two_sample_proportion_test)

Uber A/B tests a checkout tweak with n = 1,000,000 riders per arm. Control conversion = 8.00%. Treatment conversion = 8.10% (10 basis-point lift).

  • p-value = 0.0093 — very statistically significant.
  • 95% CI for the lift = [+0.025pp, +0.175pp].
  • Relative lift = ~1.25%.

So: yes, the change "works." But would you put 8 weeks of engineering, support docs, and on-call risk behind a launch that nudges conversion from 8.00% to 8.10%? That decision is not statistical — it is a product decision.

4 · What to measure

Always report three numbers, never one: (1) point estimate of the lift, (2) the 95% CI for the lift, (3) the practical threshold the team set BEFORE running the test (e.g., "we will only ship if relative lift ≥ 2%"). The pre-registered threshold is the hypothesis agent's "decision implication" turned into a number.

5 · How to decide (decision tree)
  • CI lower bound > practical threshold → SHIP.
  • CI straddles 0 → DO NOT SHIP (no evidence of lift).
  • CI strictly above 0 but below practical threshold → DO NOT SHIP (statistically real, practically too small).
  • CI strictly above 0, point estimate at threshold but CI is wide → RERUN with more traffic or higher MDE.
6 · Common mistake

Quoting only the p-value. "p < 0.05" tells the room nothing about the size of the effect. Always pair p with the CI and the pre-registered threshold.

11.3 — What to Do When the Experiment Is Inconclusive (a real APM situation)

1 · Original idea (from the Uber guide)

Sections 03 and 04 cover the "win" and "lose" decisions. But the guide skips the most common outcome: the test is inconclusive — CI straddles zero, p > 0.05. What does the APM say in standup?

2 · Simple explanation (DSC 152 style)

From DSC 152 Level 7: "fail to reject H0" is not "H0 is true." It is "we lacked the evidence to convict." That maps directly to product: "the test does not give us a green light" — it does not say the change has zero effect.

The hypothesis agent's "ambiguous zone" applies here: when the difference is too small relative to noise, classify the result as insufficient evidence rather than no effect.

3 · Concrete business example

You ran a 2-week A/B on a new map UI. Lift = +0.6pp on completed trips. p = 0.18. CI = [-0.3pp, +1.5pp]. The PM partner asks: "So… ship or kill?"

4 · What to measure (experiment-designer Step 4c)

Three diagnostics decide the next move:

  1. Was the test underpowered? Re-compute the MDE you can detect at the n you ran. If your achievable MDE is bigger than the lift the team cares about, the test was too small.
  2. Is there a guardrail flashing red? Even with no lift on primary, a degraded guardrail means kill.
  3. Are subgroups telling a story? If a single segment is moving a lot in either direction, that's the next test.
5 · How to decide
Underpowered?Guardrails OK?Subgroup signal?Decision
YESYES—EXTEND the test (more traffic or longer runtime)
NO (well-powered)YESNOKILL — true null is the most likely truth
NOYESYESSHIP TARGETED to the winning segment, kill broadly
—NO (degraded)—KILL — no upside and visible downside
6 · Common mistake

Calling an inconclusive test a "win" because the point estimate was positive. The CI straddles zero — you do not know whether the change helped, hurt, or did nothing. Resist the temptation.

11.4 — Why SUTVA Violations Bias Your CI (the math behind Section 01's claim)

1 · Original idea (from the Uber guide)

Section 01 says supply leakage breaks A/B tests. But never shows the math.

2 · Simple explanation (DSC 152 style)

The two-sample test assumes treatment and control are independent samples drawn from their respective populations. When drivers leak from control to treatment (because the discount in treatment makes those rides more attractive to drivers), the control's conversion drops not because of randomness, but because its underlying population effectively changed. The test's "noise" model becomes wrong, and the CI it computes is centered on the wrong place.

DSC 152 Level 5 connection: this is exactly the violation that makes "two independent samples" invalid — the same idea behind why we use a paired test when the same users are measured twice.

3 · Concrete business example (real numbers from stats_helpers)

Simulated A/B with n = 5,000 per arm. True treatment conversion = 12%. True control conversion = 10%. So the true lift = +2.0pp.

  • Clean run (no leakage): observed lift = +1.54pp, 95% CI = [+0.32, +2.76]pp. Includes the truth.
  • With supply leakage (control conversion drops 1pp because drivers got pulled away): observed lift = +3.24pp, 95% CI = [+2.07, +4.41]pp. The truth (+2.0pp) is now outside the CI.

The CI is no longer trustworthy — the test gave the treatment 1.7pp of credit it did not earn. Ship-decisions made on this CI will look like wins and degrade unit economics in production.

4 · What to measure
  • Diagnostic metric: driver utilization in BOTH arms, side by side. If control's utilization drops while treatment's rises, you have leakage.
  • Design fix: randomize at the city or time-window level (geo split, switchback) instead of at the rider level.
5 · How to decide

If the leakage diagnostic shows control degraded by > 0.5σ of its baseline noise, throw out the rider-level CI and rerun with a geo split. Do not "adjust" the CI — the bias direction is unknown ex-ante.

6 · Common mistake

Reporting the rider-level CI as if it were valid because it is "what the test produced." The test produced a number — the question is whether the assumptions behind that number held. Here they did not.

11.5 — Why Sample Size Explodes When You Want a Smaller Lift (intuition behind n ∝ 1/MDE²)

1 · Original idea (from the Uber guide)

Section 07's calculator works but doesn't show the shape of how n scales with MDE. APM candidates need to internalize this curve so they don't propose impossible experiments.

2 · Simple explanation (DSC 152 style)

From DSC 152 Level 12 (power deep dive): for a fixed α and power, sample size scales as n ∝ 1/(MDE)². Halve the lift you want to detect → n quadruples. Cut the lift by 10× → n grows 100×. This is not a tweak — it is the dominant cost driver of every experiment.

3 · Concrete business example (real numbers from stats_helpers.sample_size_proportion, baseline = 10% conversion)
MDE (relative lift)n per armvs MDE = 10%
10%14,7491×
5%57,760~4×

If you only have 30,000 riders/week through the affected flow, you can detect a 10% lift in two weeks but a 2% lift would take six months. That is a roadmap conversation, not a stats conversation.

4 · What to measure
  • Pre-experiment: baseline rate, daily traffic into the flow, the smallest lift the business actually cares about.
  • Output of power calc: required n per arm, expected runtime in days. If runtime > 8 weeks, the design is not viable as a standard A/B.
5 · How to decide

If runtime is too long, choose ONE of: increase MDE (accept that we'll only catch bigger wins), use a more sensitive metric (continuous > binary), use a paired/quasi-experimental design that reduces variance, or simply ship without an A/B and use rollback as your safety net.

6 · Common mistake

Promising the team a 1% MDE detection on a small surface "in two weeks." The math says no. Push back politely with the table above.

11.6 — Heterogeneous Treatment Effects & Simpson's Paradox in a Marketplace

1 · Original idea (from the Uber guide)

Pitfall 4 in Section 08 mentions "heterogeneous treatment effects" but does not show one. Without an example, candidates miss that the AGGREGATE can be neutral while a SEGMENT is bleeding.

2 · Simple explanation (DSC 152 style)

From DSC 152 Level 5 ("the big picture"): the test you run depends on what the data looks like. When the population is a mixture of subgroups with different baselines and different responses, the aggregate metric is a weighted average — and weighted averages can hide opposite-sign effects in the components. This is the marketplace version of Simpson's paradox.

3 · Concrete business example (real numbers from stats_helpers.two_sample_proportion_test)

10,000 riders per arm, split into power users (1,000 riders, 30% conversion) and casual users (9,000 riders, 5% conversion).

  • Overall lift: −0.90pp, p = 0.013 → looks "barely negative, probably noise."
  • Power users only: 0.00pp, p = 1.000 → completely flat.
  • Casual users only: −1.00pp, p = 0.001 → real, statistically clear drop.

If you only look at the aggregate, you might call this "noisy null" and ship. The casual segment — your largest by volume — is actually being damaged.

4 · What to measure
  • Always slice the primary metric by your TWO most-important user segments BEFORE looking at the aggregate.
  • Use logical_validator.validate_group_balance() to confirm allocation isn't itself imbalanced (the demo above passed: 4900 vs 5100 → ratio 1.04).
  • If a segment moves and the aggregate doesn't, the segment wins — investigate further.
5 · How to decide

If at least one important segment shows a clear directional effect (CI fully on one side of 0), pause the rollout, dig into the mechanism, and decide whether to ship-by-segment instead of all-or-nothing.

6 · Common mistake

Defending an aggregate-neutral readout as "the experiment said no effect." A well-designed experiment can show a true null on the average AND a real harm on a subgroup. The job of a PM is to look for both.
