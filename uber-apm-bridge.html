01

Why Uber Is a Unique A/B Testing Playground

Most stats classes teach A/B testing as if you have one population: "some users see version A, others see version B, look at the means." Uber breaks this because it is a three-sided marketplace with two distinct user populations whose actions affect each other in real time.

The Three Sides You Must Mention
  • Riders (demand) — care about price, ETA, ride quality.
  • Drivers (supply) — care about earnings per hour, downtime, surge predictability.
  • Uber as the platform — cares about contribution margin per trip, retention on both sides, marketplace efficiency (how often a request gets matched).

If the interviewer asks "would you launch X?", your structured answer must show how X affects EACH of the three sides. Andy Walner's Medium piece calls this out explicitly: "address specifically how a change would affect supply (drivers), demand (riders), and Uber's bottom-line."

Why Vanilla A/B Tests Break Here — and Where Your DSC 152 Knowledge Wins

Three things make Uber's experimentation harder than a textbook A/B test:

  1. SUTVA violation. If you give 50% of riders in San Diego a discount, you bid drivers AWAY from the control 50%. Treatment leaks into control through shared driver supply. Your DSC 152 hypothesis test ASSUMES independent samples — that breaks. Mention this and you sound senior.
  2. Highly skewed metrics. Trips per user, revenue per user, surge revenue — all heavy-tailed. A few power users move the mean. This is exactly why Lab 2 had you compare the t-test to the sign test and bootstrap CI on Gamma data. You already KNOW the t-test misbehaves on small-n skewed data.
  3. Network effects. A change that increases bookings can DEGRADE the experience because surge fires more often. Looking at one metric (bookings) without a guardrail (ETA, surge frequency) is rookie behavior.

Translation: every concept you are studying for Quiz 1 has a marketplace-shaped twin in this interview. Sections 3-6 walk through the mapping concretely.

Case Walkthrough: "Should Uber Roll Out a 25% Discount in San Diego?"

This is the canonical Uber APM analytical case — pulled directly from Andy Walner's article. Below is how you walk an interviewer through it using your DSC 152 toolkit.

Prompt

"Uber wants to release a 25% discount to attract new riders in San Diego. How would you decide whether to roll it out?"

Step 1 — Clarify the goal (always do this first)

Ask: "Are we optimizing for new-rider acquisition only, or for total contribution margin? Over what time horizon — 30 days or 90 days?" This single question shows the interviewer you are not racing to numbers.

Step 2 — Hypotheses (the DSC 152 Level 3 move)

H0: 25% discount has NO effect on the chosen North Star (e.g., 30-day repeat rate of new riders).
H1: 25% discount changes the 30-day repeat rate.

Two-sided is fine — "no effect" includes "discount cannibalizes margin without lifting repeat." If the interviewer presses, switch to one-sided ("we expect lift > 0") to maximize power.

Step 3 — Pick a unit of randomization (the SUTVA fix)

Do not randomize at the rider level — drivers are shared supply. Two cleaner options:

  • Geo split. Randomize at the city or neighborhood level — San Diego ZIP codes A/B/A/B. Pro: clean SUTVA. Con: tiny sample (you might have ~30 ZIPs).
  • Switchback experiment. Run discount for an hour, off for an hour, alternating. Pro: city is its own control. Con: carryover bias (drivers who came online for the discount hour stay around).

This is exactly the "two-sample / paired" decision you learned in Level 5 of the companion — but with a marketplace twist.

Step 4 — Pick a primary metric and guardrails

RoleMetricWhy
PrimaryContribution margin per acquired rider over 30 daysDiscounts only earn back if those riders stick around.
Guardrail 1p95 ETAIf demand spikes, supply shortfall hurts ETA — bad rider experience.
Guardrail 2Driver earnings per hourIf discount lowers gross bookings per trip without volume offset, drivers earn less.
Counter% of "new" riders who are returning churned riders gaming the offerCatches a known abuse pattern.

Step 5 — Sample size and power (use the calculator below)

Suppose baseline 30-day repeat rate is 40%, and you want to detect a 2-percentage-point lift (40% → 42%). You need a LOT of riders because the lift is small relative to the noise. Your DSC 152 power formula gives you the answer — Section 7 has an interactive calculator.

Step 6 — Read the result like a statistician, decide like a PM

If the test returns a 95% CI of [+1.2 pp, +2.8 pp] for repeat rate AND no guardrail breach AND positive contribution margin, ship it. If the CI straddles zero, do NOT ship — even if the point estimate is positive. This is the connection to Level 6: a CI that contains the null IS "fail to reject."

Pro Move — Tie Back to Quiz 1 Concepts Out Loud

"Because contribution margin per rider is heavy-tailed — a few high-value riders dominate — I'd want to also report a bootstrap CI alongside the t-test, exactly because the t-test can have inflated Type I error on small-n skewed data, like we saw in Lab 2."

That single sentence shows them you understand the WHY behind A/B testing, not just the mechanics.

04

Case Walkthrough: Driver-Side Experiment — Lifting Acceptance Rate

Prompt

"Drivers in Chicago accept only 65% of incoming requests. The product team wants to redesign the request screen to push that to 75%. Design the experiment."

Where DSC 152 lights up

  • Hypothesis (Level 3): H0: p = 0.65, H1: p > 0.65 (one-sided — we are not interested in the redesign making things worse).
  • Discrete data (Level 7c): Each request is a 0/1 — Bernoulli. Acceptance count is Binomial. The "rejection cutoff" intuition transfers directly.
  • Sample size: Use a two-proportion z-test. Alternatively, simulate it (your rbinom() + prop.test() skill from Lab 2).

Marketplace-specific traps to call out

  • Acceptance can rise without driver welfare rising. A driver might accept a long pickup, realize it, then cancel. Add a cancellation counter metric.
  • Selection bias on who is exposed. If only newly-onboarded drivers see the new screen, you measure novelty effect, not the redesign.
  • Heterogeneous treatment effect. The redesign might help low-tenure drivers and hurt high-tenure drivers. Slice the analysis by tenure buckets.

If you have time — the bandit angle

Mention that for high-frequency ranking-style decisions (which UI variant to show), Uber sometimes uses contextual multi-armed bandits instead of a fixed A/B. You probably won't be asked to design one; just naming the option signals breadth.

Case Walkthrough: ETA Accuracy Improvement

Prompt

"The ETA we show riders is off by 2 minutes on average. The ML team has a new model that should fix it. How do you know if it actually worked?"

Reframe the metric

"Off by 2 minutes" can mean (a) systematic bias — we always under-promise, or (b) high variance — sometimes 0, sometimes 8. Your inference toolkit answers different questions for each:

ConcernStat tool from DSC 152Decision
Bias (mean of error ≠ 0)One-sample t-test on (predicted − actual). H0: mean = 0.Reject H0 → there IS systematic bias.
Variance / tail riskCompare p95 of |error| before vs after. Bootstrap CI for the difference.p95 dropped → tail risk improved.
Skew (errors are not symmetric)Sign test on (predicted − actual) — does the median equal 0?If the median is positive, you systematically over-promise.

Notice you used THREE different DSC 152 ideas — t-test, bootstrap CI, sign test — to triangulate one product question. That is the level of nuance that separates "knows A/B testing" from "thinks like a PM."

Interactive — Sample Size Calculator (Two-Proportion A/B)

Use this in interviews when you say "we'd need about N users per arm to detect this lift with 80% power." The formula is from the same power family you're learning in Level 12.

Enter values and click Compute.
What's Going On Under the Hood (DSC 152 Connection)

For a two-proportion z-test, sample size per arm is:

n = (z_{α/2} + z_{β})²  ·  [p₁(1−p₁) + p₂(1−p₂)]  /  (p₂ − p₁)²

where p₂ = p₁ + MDE. The z's come from qnorm() — the same family as the qt() you used to get critical values in Level 7b. Smaller MDE → bigger n. Higher power → bigger n. Higher baseline near 0.5 → bigger n (Bernoulli variance is maximized at p = 0.5).

08

Five Pitfalls That Win You "Senior-Sounding" Points

  1. Peeking. Checking the test daily and stopping when significant inflates Type I error. Mention "I'd lock the sample size up front and not look until we hit it" — or use sequential methods like alpha-spending.
  2. Multiple comparisons. If you check 20 metrics, one will look significant by chance even if nothing happened. Bonferroni or pre-register your primary metric.
  3. Novelty / primacy effects. A new UI gets a temporary lift just because it's novel; an old one gets a temporary dip during the change. Run experiments long enough to wash this out (usually 14+ days).
  4. Heterogeneous treatment effects. A change can help one segment and hurt another while the overall mean looks flat. Always slice by city, tenure, platform, and trip type.
  5. Short-term vs long-term metric divergence. Discounts boost trips this week and tank LTV next quarter. Pair every short-term primary metric with a long-term guardrail.

Mock Prompts — Practice Out Loud, Time-Boxed

Pick three. Set a 12-minute timer for each. Your goal: clarify → hypothesize → metrics → experiment design → decision rule. Click each scenario to reveal the "what good looks like" answer.

Mock 1 — Pricing

"Should Uber raise base fares 10% in Phoenix?"

Clarify: "permanently or trial?", "preserve driver pay or take more margin?". Primary: contribution margin per trip × volume = total contribution. Guardrails: rider cancellation rate, churn (rides per rider over 30 days), competitor share. Design: geo holdout — keep one comparable city as control. Watch for elasticity asymmetry: small fare hikes can disproportionately affect price-sensitive riders.
Mock 2 — Driver experience

"How would you measure success of a new driver onboarding flow?"

Define "success" = lifetime trips per driver in first 30 days × eventual retention to day 90. Input metric = % of drivers who complete onboarding. Guardrail = quality (rider rating, cancellation rate). Counter = drivers who complete onboarding but never go online. Run cohort comparison; if you A/B, randomize at the driver-applicant level (no SUTVA issue here because applicants don't share supply).
Mock 3 — Engagement

"Trips per active rider dropped 4% week over week. Diagnose."

Walk the funnel: app opens → trip requests → matches → completed trips. Where did it drop? Slice by city, platform, new vs returning. Check exogenous factors (weather, holidays, competitor promo). Confirm the drop is statistically significant — a 4% week-over-week move could be noise; bootstrap CI for trips-per-rider tells you. Tie back: "I'd compute a 95% CI for the WoW change to confirm we're not chasing noise."
Mock 4 — Safety

"How would you decide whether to launch a 'verify rider photo' feature?"

This is a risk-asymmetric launch — Type II error (missing a real safety improvement) is far more expensive than Type I (small UX friction). Pre-register a higher α (e.g., 0.10) so you have more power. Primary: incident rate per 10K trips. Guardrail: friction (request abandonment). Counter: riders gaming photo requirements. Phased rollout — small geo first, watch for unintended consequences in low-light regions.
Mock 5 — ETA prediction

"Our ETA model predicts 5 min but the actual is 7 min on average. Is the model broken?"

Don't say "yes" — first ask: "is this bias persistent, or just a recent shift?" Compare distributions, not just means. Run a one-sample t-test on (predicted − actual) per trip. If the mean is significantly negative, the model systematically under-promises. But the better PM answer: "even if it's biased, fixing the bias may not improve booking conversion — riders might prefer a slight under-promise. I'd A/B the corrected vs uncorrected ETA on conversion."
